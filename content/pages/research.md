      <h2>AI-Assisted Discovery of Quantum Experiments</h2>
      <p>
        I develop computational methods that help discover new quantum optical experiments, from photonic entanglement generation to nonlocal gates and high-dimensional quantum states. A central goal is to move beyond manual trial-and-error design by using algorithms that search through complex experimental possibilities and return physically interpretable setups.
      </p>
      <p>
        This research combines quantum optics, graph-based representations, optimization, and machine learning. I am especially interested in methods that can reveal unexpected experimental mechanisms while still producing designs that physicists can understand, test, and refine.
      </p>
    </div>
  </article>

  <article class="research-topic">
    <div>
      <h2>AI Agents for Scientific Research</h2>
      <p>
        I study how large language model agents can participate in scientific workflows: reading literature, proposing research ideas, selecting tools, executing domain-specific searches, and turning abstract ideas into concrete physics designs. The aim is not to replace scientists, but to build systems that can expand the space of hypotheses and accelerate early-stage exploration.
      </p>
      <p>
        This direction raises both technical and conceptual questions: how agents should use scientific tools, how their outputs can be evaluated, how to keep generated ideas grounded in physics, and how human researchers can remain meaningfully in the loop.
      </p>
    </div>
  </article>

  <article class="research-topic">
    <div>
      <h2>Quantum Optics and Photonic Quantum Technologies</h2>
      <p>
        My work in quantum optics focuses on photonic experiments for quantum information, including multiphoton interference, entanglement, nonlocality, quantum gates, and structured photons. I am interested in how subtle features of indistinguishability, path information, and measurement can become practical resources for quantum technologies.
      </p>
      <p>
        This line of research connects theoretical design, experimental interpretation, and the development of new conceptual tools for understanding quantum phenomena in complex optical systems.
      </p>
    </div>
  </article>

  <article class="research-topic">
    <div>
      <h2>Knowledge Graphs, Ideas, and Scientific Creativity</h2>
      <p>
        I am interested in how scientific knowledge evolves and how machine learning can help identify promising, underexplored research directions. This includes work on forecasting impactful research topics, generating research ideas, and building representations that connect concepts across papers, methods, and physical systems.
      </p>
      <p>
        More broadly, this research asks how intelligence, computation, and physical principles can be combined to support scientific creativity: not only solving known problems, but helping researchers notice new ones.
      </p>
    </div>
